Automation Help Site

From broken, unnavigable documentation to a fully accessible, searchable, role-aware help portal, single-handedly.

I inherited a Finance Automation help site built inside MadCap Flare, an enterprise help-authoring tool with its own styling system, scripting layer, and output pipeline. The content had been migrated in, but the migration had left structural problems: fragile scripts, broken navigation, no accessibility, and no style guide enforcement. Customer success was escalating weekly. Over two years, I completely redesigned and rebuilt it, auditing source code with AI, restructuring ~200 articles, implementing interactive onboarding experiences, and building a role-aware help system. Everything authored inside Flare, within the constraints of an enterprise tool that doesn't make anything easy.

The Situation

The Finance Automation help site had been migrated into MadCap Flare, an enterprise help-authoring platform with a steep learning curve, its own CSS-like styling system, and a scripting layer that breaks silently when anything changes. The migration itself had been complex: content moved between project structures, topic IDs remapped, stylesheets inherited dependencies from the old project. The result was a site with fragile scripts, broken navigation paths, zero accessibility considerations, and no style guide enforcement. Customer success was escalating to me weekly.

Several of the Finance Automation engineers had departed, and institutional knowledge went with them. I reconstructed understanding of what the product actually did by auditing the source code with AI tools, first Gemini Gems, then Claude Code and Cursor. Sometimes the product managers came to me because I'd become the person who understood the system end to end.

MadCap Flare presented its own challenges. Everything, every navigation component, every interactive demo, every formatting enhancement, every stylesheet override, had to be authored inside Flare's architecture. There's no exporting to hand-coded HTML. The tool constrains what you can build and how you build it. Every redesign decision I made had to be implementable within that system.

What Changed

TOC & Navigation

The side nav went from a flat, confusing dump of every topic to a properly structured hierarchy that actually reflected how users think about the product. Added new sections that never existed: Integrations, Connectors & Extensions, Reference, About. Added cross-reference links to every new topic I created. Replaced the unprofessional footer with a proper branded one: "© Redwood Software. All rights reserved." — with links to Documentation Portal, Redwood University, and Support.

Style Guide Enforcement

The Microsoft Style Guide is the industry-standard style guide for technical documentation, covering everything from capitalization and procedure formatting to inclusive language and UI element naming. It's a comprehensive document, and enforcing it consistently across ~200 articles is a non-trivial content operation. I audited every page against the style guide, corrected violations, and built a Gemini Gem (FlareGuard) to automate enforcement going forward.

Content Audit & Rewrite

I audited the entire help site using Claude Code, reading every page flagging content for me, cross-referencing against source code where documentation was wrong or missing, and identifying gaps where entire topics had never been written. The scope: every article across Balance Sheet Certification, Task Management, Journal Entry, Operation Modules, Integrations, Connectors & Extensions, Administration, and Reference.

A "Getting Started" section that read like an afterthought became a proper onboarding guide: Before you begin → Understand your role → Sign in → Home screen → Sidebar menu → Application area → Next steps (role-specific). Just like a new employee walking through their first day.

I created a centralized glossary organized by letter, added anchor links that auto-apply throughout the site, added cross-references between related topics, and cleaned up bad URLs and formatting to match Redwood's own brand style guides.

Throughout this work, I used Claude Code as more than a writing assistant. It was a diagnostic tool, auditing the HTML source, the Flare project structure, and flagging content that had silently been broken for months or years.

PDF Pipeline

Before the Flare migration, customers could download documentation as PDFs. After the migration, that capability was dropped, and customers complained about it for years. No one had been able to solve it. I worked with Cursor to build an intelligent PDF pipeline that not only generated clean PDF output from the Flare project but dynamically reformatted content and styling based on the output format: PDFs got print-optimized layouts, page breaks, and typography, while HTML got the interactive, expandable experience. Different formats, same source, different rendering logic, solved a years-long customer complaint in weeks.

Release Notes Redesign

The release notes were a sparse table, product name, version number, and a bullet list with no categorization. Customers couldn't tell what actually changed or whether a release affected them. I redesigned the format into a structured "What's New" layout with clear sections for integrations, component-specific enhancements, and tracked fixes with version IDs. I also validated every release note entry against the actual source code, because many of the previous entries were wrong or referenced features that didn't exist in that release.

The Creative Pieces

What I'm proudest of lives on the Sandbox branch, the creative work that made it past design review but couldn't all ship to production due to release timelines.

Interactive Product Simulations ("Mini-UI Demos")

The hardest part of documenting Finance Automation was that screenshots were a maintenance nightmare, they went out of date with every UI change, weren't available for features still in development, couldn't be easily translated, and didn't show how the product worked, just what it looked like statically.

So I built interactive, playable simulations of product features directly into the help pages. Users could click through the UI, click buttons that simulated real results, and learn by doing rather than by reading. I invented a fictional company, Contoso Ltd, with three employees (Preparer, Supervisor, New Employee) and populated the demos with realistic mock data that simultaneously served as onboarding for the hypothetical employee and showed best-practice product usage. Each interactive component had (i) info bubbles that redirected users to the relevant help page, so the simulation and the documentation were one continuous experience.

This came from a simple insight: the best documentation doesn't describe what a button looks like. It shows you what happens when you press it.

Role-Aware Help Experience

Finance Automation serves four distinct user roles, Preparer, Approver, Administrator, and Viewer/Analyst, and the help documentation was written for all of them simultaneously, which meant it was useful to none of them specifically.

I implemented a "Select Your Role" menu with brief descriptions under each role. Once a user selected their role, it communicated to the RangerAI support agent so that every answer it gave was scoped to that role's permissions and workflow. A Preparer asking about approvals would get a "you don't have that permission" message with a link to their actual workflow instead.

On my Sandbox branch, where I couldn't get RangerAI permissions, I built a parallel system: a "Quick Questions" menu that intelligently populated with 3+ contextually relevant questions every time a user navigated to a new page, questions specific to their chosen role. My eventual plan was a full topic-tagging system to filter every help page by role, but I wasn't able to implement that before the project ended.

Sandbox Branch: A Creative Lab

I created a dedicated Sandbox branch of the website specifically so I could design, prototype, and iterate on improvements without blocking the production release cycle. This is where the interactive demos, role-aware navigation, and most of my creative experiments lived. It's the version of the site I'm most proud of, the truest expression of what help documentation could be when you're not constrained by production timelines.

I created it because I'm someone who always wants to improve things, not because anyone asked me to. That instinct to continuously build and experiment, even when there's no immediate requirement for it, is the throughline of how I approach documentation work.

Impact

When I started, customer success was escalating to me every single week for documentation updates. When I finished, those escalations stopped. Completely. Customer success tickets were handled through Jira in a timely fashion, and no one came to me directly anymore.

My bi-weekly meetings with the Finance Automation product managers went from reactive firefighting sessions to productive roadmap discussions. Several PMs told me directly that the site had improved. A customer success agent told me the mini-TOC with anchor links was "much better than what we originally suggested and what it was before."

I went from googling how to add a MadCap Flare favicon to being the person on the team who could parse the source code, optimize DocBot, and answer product questions that even the PMs couldn't figure out. That's the difference between someone who writes documentation and who engineers it.

I had never used agentic AI before December 2025. Within six months, I had built a PDF pipeline, interactive product simulations, role-aware help, searchable release notes, and a complete site redesign, all powered by AI coding agents that were new to me months earlier. I learned, and I learned fast. That trajectory, zero to shipping in months, not years, is what I bring to every team. I don't wait to be told what's possible. I figure it out.
